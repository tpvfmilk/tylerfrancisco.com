A year ago I made myself the firm's AI champion mostly by raising my hand. Twelve months and a lot of dead ends later, here's the honest ledger: what survived contact with real projects, what quietly got abandoned, and the one rule that mattered more than any tool.

The temptation, when you're the person who "does the AI stuff," is to reach for the most impressive thing on the shelf — the autonomous agent, the multi-step pipeline, the demo that got 40,000 likes. Almost none of it held up. The workflows that stuck were embarrassingly boring, and that turned out to be the whole point.

## The setup

I run everything through a single plain-Markdown vault under version control. No proprietary database, no lock-in — just files, links, and a handful of skills that turn a repeatable chore into one command. When a workflow earns its keep, it graduates into that vault. When it doesn't, deleting it costs nothing.

That constraint — **everything is a file, everything is reversible** — did more to keep the practice healthy than any model upgrade. It meant I could try something reckless on a Tuesday and have no regrets by Friday.

## What stuck

Three patterns kept showing up in the workflows I still use daily:

- **Capture, then file.** Get the thought down first; sort it later. Friction at capture time is where good notes go to die.
- **One skill per chore.** Ingesting clippings, logging a cost estimate, drafting a post — each is a small, named, improvable routine, not a clever prompt I retype.
- **Draft-first on anything external.** The model writes the first pass; I own the send button. Always.

> The best automation I built this year doesn't feel like AI at all. It feels like the boring part of my job quietly disappearing.

## What didn't

The autonomous agents were the biggest letdown. Not because they couldn't do the work — because supervising them cost more than doing it myself. A workflow you can read top to bottom beats an agent you have to babysit, every time. I retired three "agentic" experiments and didn't miss any of them.

## The one rule that mattered

**Lowest autonomy that works.** Give a workflow read-only access before write access, a draft before a send, a suggestion before an action. It's slower to feel powerful and far faster to trust — and trust is the only thing that lets a tool survive past its demo.

Eliminate, then automate, then delegate — in that order. Most of the wins this year came from the first verb, not the last.
